I greatly agree with this statement, and I believe that Rita's initial dream of being a London nanny has swiftly been dashed by an accident which has left her completely out of her depth in a world a lot less cushy and comfortable than she'd anticipated. In the extract the writer explains that '[Rita's] been panicking ever since they turned onto the forest road.' This demonstrates to the reader that Rita is out of her depth and helps to give the impression that even though Rita has managed to disguise it from Jeannie and the children she is not at all comfortable in her position and feels vastly unprepared. The use of the verb 'panicking' serves to evoke an emotional response in the reader; most people have been in some sort of situation where they have been completely unsure and the strong word helps to provide a way for the reader to put themselves in Rita's shoes due to its connotations with negativity and her portrayal as being flustered but successfully hiding it from the children and her employer which shows the shift of her dream of London into a nightmarish situation. Later in the extract, the writer employs the use of a rhetorical question in order to present Rita's disbelief in the situation which she has been thrust into and 'How on earth she ended up here?' This further helps to demonstrate Rita's feeling that her dream has now been completely shattered as she is no longer in London and is in fact in a forest. The writer then utilises very short sentences to describe that she is in 'A forest. Of all places. She hates forests.' These short sentences place a massive amount of emphasis on the forest and her disdain for the situation that she is in. The short, snappy nature of these sentences may have been put in place by the writer in an attempt to show the speed of Rita's thoughts. She is panicking and so her mind is racing. These short sentences display this to the reader and help to further show how far she is from her London dream of a 'churning, thrilling city' and a 'cultured' environment. Furthermore, the writer uses a short single line paragraph of 'It was meant to be a London nannying job.' This now adds further emphasis to her disbelief at her current situation. The phrase 'meant to be' is useful as it demonstrates that Rita feels that this is not what she expected when she was hired as the nanny. The writer repeatedly demonstrates to the reader Rita's dream of going to London as a nanny alongside her excitement and nervousness at the prospect of it. The writer states that 'She dreamed of it longingly' which directly uses the word 'dreamed' showing her explicit desire to work as a nanny in London, the writer also uses the adverb 'longingly' in order to describe the way in which she dreamed of it as being one of great desire but also a lack of faith in her ability to reach that goal. Later in the extract the writer describes how Rita felt as she first viewed the house in which she intended to the work and that 'she could feel a tingling Sensation like the first fizz of pins and needles' which helps to Show her nervousness and therefore the value which she must place on the role. To be nervous about something you must also care about the outcome. Overall, throughout the extract there is a stark contrast between the 'cultured', 'perfect' and 'warm' environment which she envisioned and the hatred which she had for her new environment and the danger which she sees in it. This all serves to show that her dream of a lovely enjoyable London job has been suddenly and abruptly disallowed by fate.
